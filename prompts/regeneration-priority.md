# TrailVolt — Regeneration Priority

*Created: April 2026. Grounded in brand-visual-foundation.md and direct HTML audit.*

---

## Why Regenerate

The first 3 test outputs (hero-pack-main.webp, about-founder.webp, line-card-everyday.webp) were generated as pipeline verification runs — not reviewed or approved as final brand assets. The quality and brand-alignment of those outputs is unknown and assumed insufficient. This document defines the correct 5 images for the first meaningful wave.

---

## The 5 Priority Images

### Rank 1 — `hero-pack-main.webp`
**Page:** index.html — above-the-fold hero
**Why it matters most:**
This is the first frame every visitor sees. It is the single most important commercial asset on the site. If the pack does not look expensive, precise, and desirable in this image, no copy will compensate. The hero is the brand's handshake.

**What was likely weak in the test version:**
- AI image generators typically produce a generic-looking hiking or travel pack — not a distinctly modular product
- The module attachment rail (the defining feature of TrailVolt) likely did not appear as a visible, intentional design element
- The electric mint glow effect is difficult to prompt correctly — likely came out either absent, flat, or neon-saturated rather than the subtle edge-light it should be
- Ballistic nylon texture and hardware sharpness rarely survive without very specific prompting
- The 3/4 angle and shallow depth of field combination requires precise instruction

**What must be stronger:**
- The module attachment face on the front panel must be clearly visible and readable as a design feature — not just a texture
- The electric mint glow must be a *single directional light source from behind/below* — not a colour wash
- The ballistic nylon weave must be legible as a premium material — not smooth plastic
- The pack must read as 22L — structured, not slouching, not oversized
- Studio background must be pure dark navy (#0B1626), no bleed into grey or black

### Rank 2 — `modular-system-diagram.webp`
**Page:** index.html — Modular System Explainer section
**Why it matters most:**
The modularity is TrailVolt's entire product thesis. Without a clear visual of the system, visitors read text about "snap-on modules" without understanding what that means physically. This image exists to collapse that comprehension gap in under 2 seconds.

**What was likely weak in the test version:**
- Generating a clean radial layout with 8 distinct product accessories is beyond what a single prompt typically achieves — AI models tend to merge or distort small detailed accessories
- The mint connection lines are likely missing, over-styled, or look like generic arrows
- The pack centre-piece likely lacked the module attachment face
- The composition likely looked cluttered or asymmetric

**What must be stronger:**
- The central pack must be clearly identifiable — not a generic backpack silhouette
- Each of the 8 modules must be distinct and recognisable in purpose (phone, tech, power, shoe, clothing, document, bottle, rain)
- The mint glow lines from attachment points are the key visual logic — they must appear as connecting light threads, not arrows or graphic overlays
- The overall composition must feel like an Apple product diagram — clean, precise, purposeful white space

### Rank 3 — `about-founder.webp`
**Page:** about.html — Founder story section
**Why it matters most:**
The about page is the trust-building page. Press traffic (WIRED, Gear Patrol, Monocle) lands here. The founder image humanises the brand. Without a credible, authentic founder portrait, the brand reads as faceless DTC — not a product born from genuine frustration and obsession.

**What was likely weak in the test version:**
- Generic "founder at desk" tropes — corporate pose, too formal
- AI founder portraits often have uncanny valley faces or impossible hand/arm geometry
- The Dublin workshop setting is brand-specific and unlikely to have appeared correctly
- Prototype props and fabric swatches are difficult to render with visual accuracy

**What must be stronger:**
- The setting must feel like an actual workshop/studio — not a WeWork or generic startup office
- Ruslan (the founder) should be candid — working on something, looking at a prototype, not looking at the camera
- Props must include elements that ground the brand: fabric swatch, pack prototype, hardware sample, MacBook
- The lighting should feel natural and slightly raw — not retouched, not perfect
- The image must feel like it was taken by a journalist, not a brand photographer

### Rank 4 — `pack-everyday-black.webp`
**Page:** shop.html — Best Seller product card
**Why it matters most:**
This is the revenue card. The "Best Seller" label means more units ship from this card than any other. The product image must complete the sale — it's the last visual before the add-to-cart decision. A weak product photo here costs revenue directly.

**What was likely weak in the test version:**
- Generic black backpack without visible modular front face
- Incorrect texture rendering for 600D ballistic nylon (often comes out as smooth rubber or matte plastic)
- Lighting that flattens the pack form — no visible edge definition
- YKK zipper pulls often look generic or plasticky in AI outputs
- The 22L structured form is hard to capture — packs often look either flat or oversized

**What must be stronger:**
- The module attachment rail on the front face must be clearly visible — this is the visual differentiator from every competitor
- 600D ballistic nylon texture must be legible under the lighting — visible weave
- YKK pulls must look like matte metal hardware, not injection-moulded plastic
- The pack form must communicate 22L — not a daypack, not a hiking pack, but a precision urban volume
- Dark navy background with a slight gradient; not flat black, not grey

### Rank 5 — `line-card-trail.webp`
**Page:** index.html — Trail line card in Three Lines section
**Why it matters most:**
The Trail line card speaks to a different buyer than the Everyday: outdoor-leaning, weekend-active, wilderness-aware. Without a strong trail lifestyle image, the TrailVolt Trail 28L appears to be a product with no context. The card must make someone think "I'd take that to Wicklow on Saturday."

**What was likely weak in the test version:**
- Generic "hiker on mountain" stock photo composition — person too small, pack barely visible
- Green colourway may have rendered incorrectly (too bright, too dark, or simply black)
- Irish/Scottish terrain is specific and hard to prompt correctly — likely replaced by generic alpine
- The loaded-pack detail (compression straps, bottle holder) likely not visible
- Cinematic quality typically needs explicit focal length and framing instructions

**What must be stronger:**
- The Forest Green pack must read as Forest Green (#2D5016) — not black, not khaki, not olive
- The hiker must be mid-stride on genuine Irish terrain — not a posed summit shot
- The Rain Cover Module or Bottle Holder Module must be visibly in-use or attached
- The weather must feel Irish — overcast-to-golden-hour, not clean alpine sunshine
- The image must feel like it was taken on the actual day of a real hike — not a photoshoot

## Visual Mistakes to Avoid Across All 5

1. **Generic bag silhouettes** — any backpack that could belong to any brand fails this brief
2. **Missing module face** — the front attachment rail is TrailVolt's identity; it must appear on every product shot
3. **Smooth or plastic-looking materials** — premium gear has visible, precise texture
4. **Over-saturated mint** — the mint is a whisper, not a neon wash
5. **Posed subjects** — no one looks at the camera, smiles, or strikes a lifestyle pose
6. **Wrong colourways** — Core Black is dark matte, Forest Green is deep #2D5016, Slate Grey is warm mid-grey
7. **White or grey backgrounds** — dark navy (#0B1626) only for studio shots
8. **AI artefacts** — distorted zippers, melting hardware, impossible reflections, extra stitching lines
9. **Stock-photo composition** — if it looks like a Shutterstock image, it's wrong
10. **Overcrowded frames** — the pack is always the hero; environment is context, not competition
