## Product Thesis

**The Core Pack is not a backpack. It is a system.**

One base pack (available in three lines: Everyday 22L, Travel 30L, Trail 28L) that snaps together with 8 modular accessories — all cross-compatible — to eliminate the need for every other bag you own.

The product claim: replace 4 bags (laptop bag, travel duffel, trail pack, gym bag) with 1 pack + 8 snap-on modules.

Price from €159. 5-year warranty. Lifetime repair programme. OEKO-TEX / bluesign certified materials.

**The origin:** Founder Ruslan (Dublin) owned four bags. Constant repacking, forgotten chargers, wrong shoes. Saw a man at Dublin Airport unclip a module from his pack. Spent years building what he wished existed.

---

## Visual Thesis

**The image must make the product self-evident before any copy is read.**

Specifically:
1. The studio hero shot must communicate "premium, modular, expensive" in 2 seconds.
2. The line card images must trigger "that is my weekend / commute / trip" in 1 second.
3. The module cards must communicate "I want that specific one" in 1 second.
4. The founder/about images must trigger "this was built by a real person who actually cares."

If an image requires copy to explain what it is, it has failed.

## Customer Archetype

**Primary: The Ambitious Professional who is also an Active Person.**

They move through multiple modes in a single week:
- Monday: laptop + gym kit + commute
- Friday: airport, 4-night trip, no checked bag
- Saturday: trail, hydration, waterproofs

They are 27–42. They live in or near Dublin, London, Barcelona, Amsterdam. They have discretionary income but are not reckless — they buy fewer things but buy them better. They read Monocle and Gear Patrol. They resent gear that makes them look like a tourist.

## 10 Rules for What a Correct TrailVolt Image Must Communicate

**Rule 1: The module attachment rail is always visible in studio product shots.**
It is the brand's primary visual differentiator. If it isn't there, the pack looks like any generic backpack.

**Rule 2: Studio backgrounds are dark navy (#0B1626) — not black, not charcoal.**
Navy is the brand's universe. Black reads as sportswear. Grey reads as consumer electronics. Navy is the world TrailVolt inhabits.

**Rule 3: Electric mint is a whisper, not a shout.**
Mint appears as a single directional edge glow or as connection lines in the system diagram. It does not wash, saturate, or halo. One source of mint per image, maximum.

**Rule 4: People are in motion or at work — never posed.**
No smiling at camera. No "look at my bag" body language. The person is absorbed in their journey or their craft. The pack is visible because it's on their back or in the scene, not because they're presenting it.

**Rule 5: The environment is specific, not generic.**
Dublin Georgian terrace (not "European city"). Wicklow Mountains boggy trail (not "mountain path"). Barcelona café (not "southern European town"). The specific location IS part of the product story.

**Rule 6: Material texture must be legible.**
600D crosshatch ballistic nylon, 500D Cordura, YKK zipper pulls — these are the proof of premium construction. They must be visible. Not smooth, not shiny, not plastic-looking.

**Rule 7: The scale is human.**
22L looks right on a person's back — not huge, not tiny. A product shot that makes the pack look enormous or miniature has failed.

**Rule 8: Every lifestyle image uses the correct Line's colourway.**
Everyday = Core Black (city). Travel = Slate Grey (airport/transit). Trail = Forest Green (outdoor). This is not flexible — the colourway and environment must match the Line's positioning.

**Rule 9: Each image earns its place — no generic shots.**
The tribe member photos are of specific named people in specific named places. The about shots are from specific Dublin locations. The trail shots are from specifically Irish highland terrain. Generic-feeling images destroy the brand's credibility.

**Rule 10: The image must pass the "Gear Patrol test".**
If the image appeared in Gear Patrol's annual gear guide without a caption, would it look at home? If yes, it is correct. If it looks like a stock photo or an Amazon listing, it is wrong.

## The Three Visual Environments

### Environment 1 — Studio (Product Photography)
- Where: Seamless dark navy backdrop
- Lighting: Cinematic rim lighting, electric mint backlight
- Subject: The pack only — no people, no hands, no props
- Tone: Luxury watch commercial, not outdoor gear catalogue
- Used in: hero-pack-main, all shop product cards, all module cards

### Environment 2 — The City (Everyday / Travel Lifestyle)
- Where: Dublin, London, Barcelona, Amsterdam
- Lighting: Morning or golden hour, real ambient
- Subject: Person in motion in an urban environment — commuting, cycling, at a café, at an airport gate
- Tone: Real journalism, not brand photoshoot
- Used in: line-card-everyday, tribe-sarah, tribe-marco, tribe-nadia, about-airport

### Environment 3 — The Trail (Outdoor Lifestyle)
- Where: Wicklow Mountains, Wild Atlantic Way, Pyrenees
- Lighting: Overcast-to-golden-hour, maritime Irish weather
- Subject: Person mid-stride on a real trail, pack loaded
- Tone: Arc'teryx campaign, Patagonia editorial
- Used in: line-card-trail, tribe-aoife, tribe-tomas, about-wicklow

## What the Visuals Must Communicate: Sequenced

**First impression (hero):** "That pack looks expensive. I want to know more."

**Second impression (modular section):** "I've never seen a backpack system like that. How does it work?"

**Third impression (line cards):** "That's my environment. That's what I do on Saturdays."

**Fourth impression (shop cards):** "I want the black one. Or maybe the green. Let me see the trail version."

**Fifth impression (modules):** "I need that bottle holder. And maybe the tech organiser."

**Sixth impression (tribe / about):** "12,000 people use this. It was built by someone who actually had the same problem I have. I trust this."
